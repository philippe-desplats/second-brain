# Step 1: Why a second brain

## MANDATORY EXECUTION RULES (READ FIRST):

- 🛑 NEVER accept vague answers like "to be more organized"; probe for a concrete incident
- 🛑 NEVER write files here; collect material only
- ✅ ALWAYS anchor every question in a real, recent, specific situation
- ✅ ALWAYS reflect the owner's answers back in one sentence before moving on
- 📋 YOU ARE A PRODUCT ADVISOR, not a survey form
- 💬 FOCUS on demand reality: what broke, what it costs, what "working" looks like

## WHY THIS STEP EXISTS

Most second brains die within a month because they were built on an abstract desire for order instead of a concrete, painful, recurring problem. These questions force the owner to name the problem. The answers become the workspace charter: the document the owner (and the agent) re-reads when the system drifts.

## EXECUTION SEQUENCE:

Ask the five questions below **one at a time**, conversationally. Probe once if the answer is generic. Store answers in `{charter}`.

### Q1. The trigger

> "What happened recently that made you decide you need this? Tell me about the specific moment."

Looking for: a lost email thread, a client detail forgotten before a call, a quote rebuilt from scratch, an idea that evaporated. If the answer is abstract ("I just want to be organized"), probe: "When did that last cost you time or money?"

### Q2. The status quo

> "Where does this information live today? Walk me through what happens to a meeting note or an important email after a week."

Looking for: the real current tools (notes app, email, head, nowhere) and where information goes to die. The second brain must beat this baseline, not an imaginary one.

### Q3. Success in 90 days

> "It is 90 days from now and this system worked. What can you do that you cannot do today?"

Looking for: an observable capability ("before any client call I get a one-page brief in 30 seconds", "I never rewrite a proposal from zero"). Push until it is testable.

### Q4. The wedge flow

> "If only ONE flow worked perfectly, which one would justify the whole system? Filing emails? Meeting notes? Client context before calls? Reusable knowledge?"

Looking for: the single flow to optimize first. The launch step (step-06) will exercise exactly this flow. Everything else is secondary until this one works.

### Q5. The retrieval pain

> "When did you last lose 30 or more minutes looking for something you knew you had? What was it?"

Looking for: the retrieval pattern that matters (by client? by topic? by date?). This informs which indexes and maps deserve attention.

### Synthesize

Reflect the whole picture back in 5 lines maximum and ask for confirmation:

```
So: {trigger summary}. Today that information {status quo summary}.
In 90 days, success means {success summary}.
The one flow that must work first: {wedge flow}.
The retrieval pain to kill: {retrieval pain}.
Did I get that right?
```

Adjust until confirmed. Store the final version in `{charter}`.
